Chapter Three

Our Story.

A clay tandoor and twenty-five years of dinners in Kentlands.

We opened in 2002 with a single conviction — that fine dining Indian cuisine was a category waiting to be built in Maryland. A quarter century later, the dining room still fills six nights a week, and the kitchen still grinds its garam masala by hand each morning.

Linen on the table. Stemware. Hand-carved screens. A 24-seat private room with its own service door.

The Room

Fine dining, by design.

When the floor plan for 106 Market Street was drawn, it was drawn as a restaurant — not as a counter that happens to have chairs. Linen on the table. Stemware on the bar. Hand-carved screens shipped from Jaipur. A mural inspired by an Indian love poem along the south wall. A 24-seat private room with its own service door.

In a category dominated by buffets and banquet halls, that was — and is — a deliberate choice. Tandoori Nights is a fine dining restaurant. Take your time. Order another bottle.

"There are eighty Indian restaurants in Montgomery County. There is one fine dining Indian restaurant. That is by design."
The Kitchen

A clay tandoor. Daily-ground spices.

Every spice on the line is whole when it arrives. Cardamom is cracked the morning of service. Cumin is dry-roasted, cooled, and milled. Coriander is hand-toasted. Our garam masala, our tandoori masala, our chaat masala — all blended in-house. Nothing comes from a jar.

The tandoor itself is a traditional clay vessel — the kind sunk into the ground in the Indus Valley two thousand years ago. Lined annually. Tended throughout service. There is no shortcut to a 900-degree wall of radiant heat, and we are not interested in finding one.

It is slower. It is more demanding. It is the only way the food tastes the way it does.

The Pantry

Less oil. All natural. Halal.

Indian food has a reputation in this country for being heavy. It is not — at least not when it is cooked the way it is meant to be cooked. We use markedly less oil than the conventional curry house. We do not finish with industrial cream. We do not use food coloring. All meat in our kitchen is halal-certified. Our paneer is made in-house, twice weekly. Vegetables are whole on arrival.

A lot of guests tell us they sleep better after eating here. We are not surprised.

A Quarter Century

A timeline.

2002

The doors open at 106 Market Street.

The clay tandoor is fired for the first time in Kentlands.

2008

Catering reaches one hundred events a year.

Weddings, board dinners, and Diwali parties across Montgomery County.

2014

The private dining room opens.

Twenty-four seats with their own service door, used for rehearsal dinners and corporate evenings.

2020

Online ordering launches.

Direct pickup and delivery on Toast — and the dining room never closed.

Today
